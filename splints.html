        <h2>Immobilization Techniques: Splints</h2>

        

        <p>A focused visual guide to common upper and lower extremity splinting required during an on-call shift, emphasizing material use and proper positioning.</p>


        <div class="splint-card">

            <h3>Volar Forearm Splint</h3>

            <p><strong>Primary Use:</strong> Distal radius fractures, carpal injuries, soft tissue wrist injuries. <br> 

               <strong>Key Tip:</strong> Ensure adequate padding over the ulnar styloid and maintain a slight wrist extension (10-20 degrees).</p>

            <p><a href="#" style="color: var(--medical-blue);">View Step-by-Step Visual Guide</a></p>

        </div>


        <div class="splint-card">

            <h3>Sugar-Tong Splint</h3>

            <p><strong>Primary Use:</strong> Elbow and forearm fractures (e.g., highly unstable distal radius or both bone forearm fractures). <br>

               <strong>Key Tip:</strong> Prevents pronation/supination. The elbow must be maintained at 90 degrees of flexion.</p>

            <p><a href="#" style="color: var(--medical-blue);">View Application Video</a></p>

        </div>


        <div class="splint-card">

            <h3>Posterior Long-Leg Splint</h3>

            <p><strong>Primary Use:</strong> Tibia fractures, unstable ankle fractures, knee dislocations (temporary). <br>

               <strong>Key Tip:</strong> Include the entire foot and maintain the ankle in neutral dorsiflexion. Must be well molded to the calf and heel.</p>
